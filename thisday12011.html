1.12.11

On January 12, Mr. Mézot, a painter of artistic scenes, dreams that two young people from the firm of Mariage Brothers, makers of fine teas, are taking him somewhere in a taxicab. He feels like a prisoner; when the cab stops on the quai de l'Horloge, he opens the door and leaps out. He rushes towards a poor madman, who is in the process of hanging himself from a lamppost. "Give me that rope!" shouts Mr. Mézot. The madman gives it to him. Mr. Mézot knots the rope around his ankles. "This is how you tie yourself up," he explains, in order to save the stranger.

In the second part of the dream, it's the Mariage Brothers themselves who congratulate Mr. Mézot warmly. "A son-in-law who makes tea is a born ally," one of them says, citing a Turkish proverb. Well, yes, that's right, Mr. Mézot says to himself just before he wakes up, seeing, perhaps, this poor sick person attached to a lamppost as a kind of human teabag. That morning, Mr. Mézot will have time only to reflect that he didn't make tea at all; rather the opposite. Soon he will be obliged to think of other things: it's his wedding day.
