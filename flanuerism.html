FLANUERISM

     “Flanuer”, a French word meaning stroller, saunterer or loafer comes from the verb, also French, “flaner” meaning, “to stroll.”  The word “flaneur” was popularized by Charles Beaudelaire, who developed a derived meaning, that of  “a person who walks the city in order to experience it.”  Charlie Beau, as his closest friends called him, was already in his thirties when Georges-Eugène Haussmann, during Napoleon III’s “Second Empire,” built the grand boulevards of Paris we know today.  The grand boulevards helped create a more beautiful and sanitary city and would make it much easier to move troops and artillery around (see WWs I+II).  The city was opened up and was now highly strollable. Beaudelaire would vomit in disgust if he saw the sorry state of strolling in the States.

     Today very few of us get around without an automobile.  Many supposed gentlemen are quick to pipe up “I’ll drive!” when a plan for an outing is formed.  For those without cars, the feeling of being a second-class citizen is close to the surface.  All over television there are ads for cars, full of images extolling the freedom and excitement that car ownership brings!  You can load all your mp3s onto your navigation system and use your 12 cupholders to hold every variety of coffee Starbucks brews!  There is no need to leave your car ever again because along with these conveniences your internal combustion engine is equipped with a refrigerated glove box and 4 dvd players plus 16 speakers and don’t forget the automated rear lift-gate, should you be tempted to actually extend and contract a muscle group.  You car will parallel park for you!  Tell it you’re hungry and it maps the route to the nearest Chili’s, where you can pick up your pre-frozen entrée without ever leaving your climate-controlled exoskeleton.

     What a real, 21st century gentlemen knows is that we, especially those of us who live in urban environments, have strayed too far from the simple pleasures embodied by the idea of the flaneur.  When Beaudelaire, or Henry Miller or Ernest Hemingway wanted to get across town they did it in a fashion that allows for interaction.  Walking, bicycling, trolley, subway, bus, even a moped or scooter, these forms all allow us to see and be seen.  To let the sun hit our face and the wind blow sand into our mouths.  We may spit and gag a little, but isn’t that what makes being alive wonderful?  Thinking “Did I just swallow a bug?  Oh well, a little bug never hurt anyone….Or did it?  Why yes, I remember reading about that spider that laid eggs in that gentleman’s handkerchief and when he went to discreetly blow his nose, the eggs were inhaled and into his lungs and the eggs hatched and spiders filled his trunk….”
 
     When you confine yourself to a car, you lose some of your humanity.  Sealed in behind shatter-proof glass and sound-insulated doors, how can you complement a fellow gentleman on his pocket square or slowly gaze from behind sunglasses at the back of a young lady’s thigh as it is revealed when a brisk wind lifts the hem of her skirt ever so slightly and she doesn’t notice because she is talking on her cell phone while carrying bags from shopping?  Yelling through your open window at the gentleman?  Why, you could startle him and cause him to trip, sending him tumbling into some puddle of filth.  And the young lady?  You should be keeping your eyes on the road, sir!  If instead of driving you were walking or cycling, you could pause and in a graceful move excuse yourself and pay your compliment to the gentleman, or act as if you are confused as to what street you’re on, all the while appreciating the shape and fine adornment of the young lady’s leg. 

     By eschewing 4-wheel transport for 2-wheel and foot transport, you open yourself up to a vibrant world of street-level life.  Outdoor café diners’ plates are now only feet from your nose and mouth.  Flowers in outdoor racks tempt you with their fragrance, “my sweetheart would love a few lilies for her bedroom….”  In this way you stay connected to the city and its people.  You overhear the conversations and idle chatter of fellow strollers, moving slowly or quickly if you want, not forced to keep up with the traffic.  Try ditching your car for a week, for a month.  Then see if you’re life has become any worse.  My guess, dear readers, is that it will become better in ways you couldn’t have imagined.
