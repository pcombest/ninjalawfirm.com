He's lying on a train track. No. Only his head is over one of the rails, the rest of his body stretched out to the side of the track, on top of the whitish gray stones. It's strange: the left side of his body gives the impression of sleep's relaxation, but the other half seems rigid, stiff, as if he were already dead. In the upper part of the frame I can see a hillside of firs (yes, firs!) and on top of the hill a group of pink clouds, as if from a Golden Age dusk.

10.12.11

Battersea Power Station. South London.

Some of the most famous rap/Hype Williams videos were filmed here. Busta's Put Your Hands Where My Eyes Can See. Nas's Hate Me Now. Missy's The Rain (Supa Dupa Fly). We interviewed Hype about shooting on location in Battersea.

NLF: Who's your favorite rapper?

HW: Muhammad Ali.

NLF: Did you have to get a permit to film in Battersea?

HW: No.

NLF: What did Battersea mean to the rap community?

HW: You know, it was this coming out for black people. We came out...covered in soot. The press thought we were filming black-on-black face. We weren't sure if we'd finish filming from all the backlash. Then we had Nas and Puff crucified on top of that.

NLF: Puff didn't like that?

HW: No Puff didn't. I asked him before he got on the cross if he was cool and he said yeah. But after the first edit he said no way. Whatever, we cut his part on the cross. It was Nas's song anyways.

NLF: Were the working conditions dangerous inside?

HW: Hell nah! We had top of the line generators pumping electricity. Kind of funny since we were in a coal power station.

NLF: So why all the soot?

HW: I like to keep it authentic. I didn't want to change too much. I was really into Von Trier and Gummo at the time. I wanted to bring a Dogma 95 aesthetic.
