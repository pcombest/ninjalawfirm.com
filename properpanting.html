PROPER PANTING

     Humans didn’t always wear pants.  Early man lacked the sharp tools and techniques necessary for turning organic material into woven cloth.  Without keys, wallets and cell phones early humans had no need for pockets or pants in which to place those pockets.  A thick layer of hair was all the heat insulation those humans needed.  Eventually humans stood straighter, hair became thinner and thinner, technology and necessity converged and the first pant was born.

     The first pants were crude, to say the least.  In the tropical climates of southeast Asia, in what is now Indonesia, early humans used fish skins to create elaborately patterned trousers.  Entire halves of tuna and mackerel would be skinned and their tough exteriors cleaned and sewn together to create a spectacular precursor to the well-known herringbone pattern of today.  Silk and spice traders brought word of these new types of durable leg coverings and in Europe craftsmen and women began using sheep and lamb skins to make durable bottomwear.  Later innovators used the intestines and stomachs of the same animals to fashion the first overalls, or as they were known in Bavaria, “Überalles.” 

     Those who could afford pants generally owned two pairs: one for everyday; for work, and one that was never worn except to be buried in.  These “death pants”, as they were known, were similar to normal pants except for one difference; the backside was cut out.  The early tailors of Europe were largely members of a now extinct religion that believed the soul escaped through the ass after a person died.  Being buried in pants without an ass-scape hatch guaranteed that the soul would be stuck in rectal purgatory.  Tailored pants remained a luxury through the middle ages and the higher up you wore your pants, the higher your social position.  The Hapsburgs all wore trousers up to their nipples. Those that could only afford one pair of pants or no pants at all made due with their creativity.  A burlap sack of flour?  A durable, albeit incredibly itchy, bottom layer.  In the vast forests of northern of Scandinavia, pine branches were woven together to protect the legs.

     Today, modern manufacturing techniques have led to the relative depreciation in the value of pants.  Most people have several pairs, in different materials, colors and patterns.  Nobody would mistake you for royalty if you casually mentioned that you got a new pair of slacks.  But with ubiquity comes disregard.  Now that anyone with 10 bucks can have a decent pair of corduroys, people don’t take as much care in the appearance of their pants.  Jeans are left to dissolve on the body of their wearer, threads stretched across knees and thighs, barely holding intact.  Sweatpants at all hours of the day and in any setting.  But the worst and most egregious misuse of pants is the unreasonably popular practice of sagging.

     To those unfamiliar with this term, sagging is when a young man will wear his pants far below the waist they were intended to hug, so far that his underwear is exposed for any unsuspecting onlooker.  When I see a young man, underwear exposed and loosely bunched between his waistband and the under part of his ass cheeks, I wonder; where has civilization failed this potential gentleman?  At what point was he given the impression that presenting his backside to the world in this way was a good idea?  Are images on television to blame?  Or magazines?  Perhaps those saggy panted hip-hop artists are promoting this idea that ill-fitting pants are de riguer?  If this problem were limited to one demographic of people, maybe there would be an easy culprit to identify.  From my research, however, there seems to be no sub-set of the population not affected by this scourge.  Pants, it should go without saying, ought to fit the wearer.  It's called a waistband for a reason, people!  If you are out for an evening stroll and notice a soft breeze tickling your tuchus, your pants are sagging, Jack, and it's time to get a belt and get that gabardine up above your butt.  Now doesn't that feel better?  Of course it does.  

     The vast majority of sagging pants are jeans.  Ever since Levi and Strauss introduced their durable denim to frontiersmen of the west, jeans have been growing in popularity.  Now jeans are the go-to for millions of Americans.  Besides looking shabby saggy jeans prevent the aspiring gentleman from performing innumerable tasks that would result in his personal betterment.  If you're worried about your pants falling down around your ankles, will you be able to confidently stride up to a lovely woman on the street and ask her for a moment of her time?  Will you be able to help a nice young woman in your apartment building get a table in and out of the elevator?  Will you be able to chase that bus to get to your appointment on time?  The answer to all three of these questions is, emphatically, no.  

     Your pants should be the last thing you worry about and they should only come down when you want them to, not because you raised your hand to hail a cab.  Proper panting is, in essence, wearing pants that fit you.  Wearing your jeans above your ass and keeping your underwear undercover.  But proper panting isn't just about the physical appearance of your jeans, slacks, trousers, or kilt.  Proper panting is having the balls to zip up, button up, throw a belt around your waist and tell the world, "I'm a motherfucking gentleman."
