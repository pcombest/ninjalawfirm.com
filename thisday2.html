2.1.10

Ragtime was a household favorite. Most parents forbade the noise pouring out from the red-light district, but Lily's found it catchy. Her father would swing her around by the feet as the sounds of Jelly Roll Morton blared out of the family phonograph. Her mom always sat in the rocking chair and said, "You two should be in the pictures."
Dad preferred Al Jolson to anything. He once smeared shoe polish all over his face and performed Mammy at the town's annual picnic. Lily was embarrassed, so she walked back home, went straight to her room and read until she fell asleep. When she awoke the next morning her book was creased and her father still had polish behind his ears.

2.5.10

Peters' favorite hip-hop duo, he decided when he was 17, is The Beatnuts.

The day before his 21st birthday, Peters found himself listening to a message left by Psycho Les & Juju as he walked through the mall arguing with his girl, Michelle...

Voicemail: Yo P! It's Les and Ju. ¡Hemos oído que es su cumpleaños mañana! Dirty Boots. We're taking over!

Michelle: Hang that shit up!
Peters: You hang it up. This mall's noisy. I can't hear shit!
Michelle: I'm calling my sister to pick me up.
Peters: Not on my phone. I got messages to listen to.
Michelle: We're not doing nothin' for your birthday tomorrow.

That next day, Michelle stayed at her sister's house and watched re-runs of Plaid Style Day Catcher (Peters' least liked show), while The Beatnuts came through Peters' house in East Rutherford for an impromptu house party. Peters was in awe, due mainly to the sheer amount of children and ballons they brought.

2.15.10

Police radios were buzzing and fuzzing as they took Sean Paul off the stage for irreverent dance moves while performing amidst 7,000 college students and a streak of spring humidity in Huntsville, Alabama. The weather report that morning said temperatures could reach fire.

2.19.10

Around 1855 in North Carolina a shoot-out took place between some white settlers and the last band of hostile Indians left hiding out in the mountains. The settlers won, captured the Indian chief and hung him from the nearest tree. His name was Skyuka. The shoot-out was talked up over the years until it got to be known as the Battle of Round Mountain. With the Indians gone, the railroad company began laying a single track into the mountains, and four miles uphill of a little town called Landrum they built a passing station so a train going downhill could pull over and let an uphill one by. Next to the track they built a few houses to shelter the railroad workers during the winter. Then they started thinking about a name for this new town.
Someone suggested that they call it Skyuka after the lynched Indian chief, but the older folks didn’t take to the idea of naming their town after a man they had once set swinging from a tree, so they called it Tyron City instead, after Tyron Peak which towered above them. In 1891 they dropped the city part and settled for plain Tyron, and that’s how my home town was born.

- Nina Simone, from her autobiography, I Put A Spell On You

2.23.10

Kandinsky shot a glare in Klee’s direction as he tapped the face of his watch.
"Let’s get a move on."
"Gee Vassily, vat’s the rush?"
They had just seated themselves at a café in Neuilly-sur-Seine, alongside, but not with, thirteen noisy, armed Germans.
The one garson was speaking with a regular at the bar and had yet to acknowledge their presence, much less make his way over. Kandinsky really wasn’t feeling it.
"PAUL! Paul Klee! Heiliger Bimbam!" one of the Germans exclaimed from the overcrowded table. The table fell silent, as all of the German ears perked up and necks stretched like an owl scanning the field for a mouse.
"Heiliger Bimbam, it’s Axel." Klee whispered into his hand beneath all the attention. He handed Kandinsky a pocket sketchbook and a gold lighter under the tablecloth and stood up.
The highly excitable German approached the table with medals dangling.
"Paul! How have you been? It’s me, Axel!"
The German looked as if he was going to hug Klee but Paul quickly extended his hand. The familiarity caused the table of Germans to lose their gaze and return to their general ruckus.
"Not well."
"Sorry to hear that."
"What brings you to Paris?"
"Don’t you know?"
"I’m afraid not."
"We came for you."
"For me?"
"The Fuhrer wishes to obtain your services."
"He can’t have them."
"I’ll let him know." Scoffing. "Personally!" The German threw his right arm up in salute and clicked his heels.
"Do that Axel. You do that."
He left Klee standing next to the table where Kandinsky was seated and returned to his cohorts. The owls appeared again.
