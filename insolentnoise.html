I N S O L E N T
N O I S E
by Roger Thornhill, Esq.

CLARKS DESERT BOOT

Darkness.  Intolerable.  Imperceptible.  A bloody nuisance.  What's worse is the headache, no doubt precipitated by the irksome pressure politely issued by the blindfold.  If I had a say in this pickle, I'd demand to see the trainers on the imbecile who tied it; I'll wager he's the inferior breed - the breed that constructs knots when the time comes to lace up.  Plebeians, the lot.

I'm sitting shoulder-to-shoulder in what I can only surmise is the soiled cabin of a prisoner transport vehicle.  The chap to my left prays fearfully to his creator.  His Cairene come out stammered, fused with pathetic, gutless bursts of the boo-hoos.  Get a hold of yourself, man - we're only en route to our execution.  This poor bastard needs some Keynes in his life:

‘The long run is a misleading guide to current affairs.  In the long run we are all dead.’

- John Maynard Keynes, economist, 1st Baron Keyenes of Tilton.  Father of the soon-to-be-late Roger O.  Thornhill.

Okay, so he wasn't the real patriarch of the Thornhill family, but I'd like to think so; my true originator was a drunk, failed pugilist, one vainglorious womanizer and a loathsome thief who departed this world with outstanding debts, leaving behind an irrational bitch of a wife and a squadron of pugnacious misfits and scalawags, including yours truly.

We decelerate as the road beneath devolves; pavement becomes dirt, rocks - a bumpy bloody jaunt.  Heavy winds assault the cabin, whipping mercilessly against our conveyance - that's Egyptian desert I smell, gusting through the ducts above.  I feel the chap beside me begin to tremble, I smell the poor bastard wetting his trousers.  We must be close.

The barrel of a Kalashnikov nudges me on my feet - its possessor's breath only intensifies the sweltering heat.  I land the dirt, the crepe soles of my teak-toned Clark’s Desert Boots cushion my fall.  Nine perilous years old and going strong.  Name another boot with the same durability as the perennial brand of English-powered trekkers and you too could be the proud owner of the UK's 35th largest (private) firm.

I'm walking blind, surrounded by the grunts, groans, prayers and sobs of my fellow captives - The Reaper has come to collect and his offerings are beginning to lose it.  What little fear that twists within my being I transmit through a mild erection; ever since my holiday in Timor-Leste I've since mastered the rare art of fear-to-arousal transference. 

My "matan dook" (that's "witch doctor" for you non-enlightened filth) is a lovely Timorese man by the name of Carl; I traded an entire consignment of Chesterfield's for the privilege of his discipline - little does the bugger know that the only thing that's Chesterfield about the fags are the packaging.  Carl's loss; my gain.

"Yallah!"  Pierces my eardrums as I'm forced to my knees, the blindfold combatively removed. 

I'm looking at a half-dozen Berber mercenaries, witlessly operating under the charge of one Berwick L.  Winthorpe, an AWOL British Commander-turned-warlord living under the nom de guerre Nassor Lefèvre.  I received an anonymous tip that Winthorpe/Lefèvre was spotted in Cairo some three weeks prior.  The bounty on his head is worth two million pounds and rising - and I aim to claim it.  See, Winthorpe/Lefèvre is the very reason I'm in this pickle - 

"I've got a clear shot at your head, Winthorpe/Lefèvre."  I uttered nine hours prior. 

The Walther's grip felt warm even in my gloved palm; two of Winthorpe/Lefèvre's heavies laid motionless on the wood behind me - the reason for my pistol's rise in temperature.

Winthorpe/Lefèvre sat facing his window.  His hair colored jet-black, falling at his shoulders, palpably overwhelming his slender frame.  The man had abandoned a considerable amount of weight - most do when converting to crime and living life on the lamb.  He was the picture of stillness, barely moved his lips to say, "Is that you, Roger?"

"Considering the fact I'm about to make two million quid?  I surely hope so.  Why don't you put your hands on the table, after do you drop the Lugar."

The Parabellum clunked the wood.  Winthorpe/Lefèvre carefully touched the desk with both palms, facing down.  He might've had his back to me, but he was watching.  Not all of us need eyes to see.  I edged toward him, scooped the pistol.  Then searched him.  I found a two-inch Smith and a Bayonet on his person.  The bastard had a gun to his head, and yet he bragged, "I'm not an easy man to find."

"Gaver from the Yard said nabbing you was next to futile.  Claimed you were the most elusive man on the continent.  I suppose that makes me the most capable."

"How much to let me walk?"

"If that's a joke, you're a dreadful comedian.  Best you stick to crime."

"Five million pounds.  In your hands in under an hour.  All you have to do is accompany me to the bank."

And that's why I'm in the desert, not because I believed him - because he kept me talking.  I can't even recall what was said after he made the offer - it didn't matter.  I was the bloody fool for engaging him when I should’ve pulled the trigger.  By the time I realized how long our exchange dragged on his mercs had reported to the hideout, rifles up.

I'm watching a short, chest-rattling burst from a Kalashnikov hammer one of the prisoners to the sand.  An eye-patched merc grips my face, trains it on the corpse - just to be sure I'm not missing anything.  Eye Patch shoves me into the dirt.  The other prisoners avert their gaze.  It's my turn to die.

On my hands and knees, sandy-eyed, wiping desert from my stubble.  I’m staring at the boots of my executioner, and there they are: knots.  Two of them.  Tighter than a sister's fanny.  And then comes the rage, stacking up, working my heart like a punching bag.  My executioner,  no doubt the same dodger who secured my blindfold before tossing me into the vehicle.  I stare at the barrel of his weapon, rising to fire, blocking out my sun.  Pull the trigger you bastard -

Shouting.  Gunfire.  The thunder of an engine.  It's bloody bedlam and I don't know why.  There's a gap in my memory bank, between the moment I was staring down the barrel of that knot-tying cretin's rifle and now is an irksome blur.  Damn you, Roger, you've gone and blacked out again. 

I'm behind the wheel of the transport vehicle.  Driving like a madman.  There's blood on my hands, but I feel no pain.  Eyes to my mirrors: prisoners in the cabin, armed for bear and trading shots with mercs - packed like sardines in a '34 Packard.  A hard left and we're in downtown Cairo - 

"Out of the way, or die!"

Traffic's a horror, but I make due - threading, exploiting both lanes.  A bullet punctures the windshield from behind.  These guns for hire mean business - so I slam the brakes.  Prisoners hold tight, bear witness as the collision modifies the '34 Packard into a present day accordion.  

The mercs come to - well, some do.  Winded and outfoxed, their mangled limbs fused with crumbled steel and devastated glass.  Prisoners raise their rifles, let their former captors have it.  A vengeful rendition of Rat-a-tat-tat echoes through Cairo's corridors.  I put Clark to gas and tear off, leaving behind a smoldering hunk of dead man and irreparable machine.

Showered, clean-shaven and freshly clothed.  I'm standing at the edge of a lonely corner, inhaling the tail end of a Dunhill and street-lit.  Across the pavement -

Snobs.  Dozens of them.  Crowding a gimcrack eatery masquerading as a four-star French cafe dubbed Arabesque.  The touristy clientele attired in newly acquired linens and silks, boasting about their 'perilous' desert tours and expert haggling methods with local merchants.  These highbrows are wining and dining to their hearts' content, oblivious to the shadow across the street, establishing a firm a grip on the Walther tucked away in the left pocket of his tan trousers.

I give my Dunhill the flick, stride across the asphalt.  A barreling taxi locks before me - don't flinch, don't freeze - just keep it moving.  In the cafe now, shoving the maître d' aside.  Walther drawn.  Safety off -

A fair girl in Levis and frayed Frye's whirls in her seat: a man in a Panama sits across.  He lifts his head - it's Winthorpe/Lefèvre and he's pissing fear.

BANG-BANG!…BANG!  Three times I shoot him.  If he isn't dead, he's well on his way.  The cowgirl screams.  Patrons make way as I march out the front doors, jump in that taxi and pass the driver enough to get me to Port Said.  By the time someone dials the law, we're outside Cairo.  Just a cabbie and his fare taking in another ravishing Arabian night. 

Looks as if I'm The Reaper for the present, and it's time I'm bloody paid...

 

 

MEMO TO: THORNHILL, R.O.
FROM: BASE-CHATHAM-RIDLEY

Roger. Roger. Stop. Look around. Where the hell are you? I have been advised by Ms. Julian to pull an M on you and request your badge and gun, as well as cut off your credit access until you report into Home.

If there is a conflict, relay thru Anouk before it is too late.

Trouble always finds its way to land directly under the soles of your shoes. I know you stomp hard and parade in new pairs across many continents, but really, Roger, this distant void of insolent noise has to stop. Let me know when you are ready to start proper.

Rendezvous: “Come clean. You need a shower.”

Until Then,
B. Waters

 

 

NB 1300

The mother's derrière was oddly shaped, like a triangle doing a handstand.  She howls in a dreadful tone of voice - a bloody earsore:

"Cállate!"

Not even flanged earplugs could hush this racket.  She repeats herself, more audible than before:

"Cállate la boca!"

And yet her child protests.  A stern mother would have the right mind to deliver a proper strike across the nose - that's how I was placed.  I turned out marvelously.  Aside from the rare act of justifiable larceny and the odd tussle with chaps more than deserving of a good hiding, I'm a model citizen.  Polished, read, benevolent.  Perhaps I should open a school: The Thornhill Academy for Proper Being. 

Better have Janice draft a memo: 'Tactics.  Inquiries.  Priorities.  The Thornhill Academy For Proper Being.  Pursue the appropriate channels and hobnob accordingly.  Pronto.'

The ferry leaves Puntarenas; Poon-tah-re-nahs as the locals love to enunciate.  Too much rolling of the tongue for my tastes.  Shoot me, I call it like I read it. 

"For you señor."

The chap with a tan slips me a piece of paper - aged parchment, 3 x 3 1/4 inches.  I glance the message while the Tan torches two Padillas.  I mumble nothing worth mentioning and trade paper for Padilla.  The Tan sets fire to the document, giving it a good toss into the roiling drink.  Ocean waters.  The damnedest thing.  Never like you see in the postcards. 

The Padilla receives a long, healthy draw.  I taste Miami as the ferry carries visitor and Costa Rican alike to the jungled peninsula...

Fifty-four foggy minutes later and I'm holding a four-inch Colt, grip burning palm like a hot cup of Turkish.  The Tan bleeds beside me.  His wound rests just above the clavicle - still issuing smoke.  I jam a small branch between his teeth.  The Tan bites hard.  His pain now on mute.

We're sheltered behind a cristobal tree.  I count five rebels closing the gap, thirty meters and counting. 

Sixty seconds to act, maybe less.  Roger, dear boy - think!

What's certain are my 1300's.  Snug and ideally cushioned.  Uncompromised amidst the most taxing of crouched positions.  And then there's the mesh toe-box, dispersing generous amounts of euphoria to the southern quarters of my trotters.

I release the Colt's cylinder: three rounds for five rebels.  Impossible to most, but I've done more with much less.

Eyes shut, five deep breaths and my fury is summoned.  My power animal's an eleventh century Piker by the name of Eric the Quarrelsom.  Ten feet between the rebels and yours truly. 

I step out from behind the cristobal, hands aimed at the gathering clouds - Colt-less. The rebels freeze, bolt-actions up and trembling: they're afraid of me and I can't say I blame them.  A moustached fellow with a lazy lower lip takes the lead.  His stride stutters fearfully.  Even if I spy a machete holstered to his hip, my eyes betray nothing. 

Moustache motions to search me - his first and final folly.  Before he can speak - before his mind can process the gesture, the revolver's in my hand. 

The Colt jumps once: moustache down.    

The rebels open fire just in time to mow down their own compadre.  I stand behind moustache: when in doubt use a shield.

The Colt jumps again: rebel down.  Three to go.  Let's even the score, shall we?

I pitch moustache's carcass toward the firing squad, knocking down the three amigos like bowling pins. 

My Colt jumps a third time: rebel down.  Two to go.  No mas bolas.  So I hack.  I'm a butcher on Sunday.  Cleaving my opposers to mincemeat.  Thunder claps.  Rain sweeps over the jungle, washing away the gore.

I cry to the heavens, releasing the fury within.  Hands to knees and gasping for air - I'm Roger again.

I hoist the Tan over my shoulder and begin the long trudge back to the docks.  The warlord is dead.  Costa Rica will sleep soundly this evening.

Back on the damned ferry and craving a shower.  Somewhere in an unnamed village the Tan rests in a hospital bed, bullet out, speeding towards a healthy recovery.  The peninsula slowly shrinks as we gain distance.

I'm four pages into a spy novel before I toss it overboard.  Who reads this rubbish?

And then she sits beside me.  Skin silky-smooth, hair jet black with subtle streaks of blonde interwoven.  I don't know her name or from where she hails, but something about this one spells t-r-o-u-b-l-e.  I feel a nudge me from beneath her elbow, recognizing that old feeling.  Time to break the ice, "Your gun is poking at my heart." 

She remains still.  Her tone is even - she's done this before.  The accent places her somewhere in the Mediterranean, Gibraltar if I had to guess.

"Stand up, Mr. Thornhill, and remain casual.  When we reach the port, I will lead you to a white sedan.  If you attempt to defy my instructions the Walther will fire.  Please nod if you understand me."

I look into her eyes, arrested.  Never before has beauty presented itself so stunningly. 

And just as instructed I nod my head.

I understand, indeed.


Zapato Del Barco

It's the damned ringing in my ears.  Constant.  Endless.  Nagging.  My head's the real problem: it's throbbing.  Like the agonizing aftermath of a headlong collision with a brick wall - whatever that feels like.  I haven't seen this many colors since my days in the Legion when that scheming bastard Jackson slipped lysergide in my Gibson.  I still owe him a slug, or two.  If I make it out of this chamber alive, I'll have my secretary add it to the agenda: 'Find that pest of a specimen Jackson and smite him for befouling my Gibson, period dot.'

While we're on the subject of smiting, my interrogator issues a right fist - his fourth.  I hear my nose break - then I feel it.  My nostrils are whistling - that means I'm still breathing.  Don't count me out just yet.

I'm staring at a duo of hoodlums.  One tall, pure brawn; the other wiry, jockey-short.  Their unremarkable apparel places them at low-level on the underworld scale.  Did I mention pea-brained?  If the Syndicate thinks I'm going to give the location of those precious coins to a couple of their nickel-and-dime bruisers, well by God they've never tangled with Roger O. Thornhill.  

Behind me: the fibrous restraints binding my wrists are beginning to give - thank heavens I shaved with a razor blade this morning.

The jockey lands a fist just above my right cheekbone.  I feel two knuckles dig into my eye.  More colors.

The brawn paces behind him, cracking his knuckles - all threat and posture.  His German lilt is heavy, impatient, "Where are the coins, Mr. Thornhill." 

Addressing me as "Mr.", it's comforting to know he realizes his place in the world. 

I don't even look at the brawn, spitting blood on the jockey's downmarket loafers instead.  I begin to laugh, my nose performing a flute solo, "My chin, aim for the chin, young man."

The Jockey swings, oblivious to the fact that I've cut myself free.  I bring my arm around - the razorblade wedged between my middle and index fingers - countering the jockey's strike.

The brawn blinks, missing that split-second interval where I've opened the jockey's windpipe.  All he sees now is his vertically challenged comrade whimpering on the floor, choking on his own crimson. 

Now let me be clear: I'm no killer.  I find the sport to be heartless, mean-spirited.  But when a man of my experience and conditioning is forced into a corner, my instincts take the stage - anything goes, really.

The brawn draws his Luger.  I hurl the chair I was tied to, deflecting the shots.  Wood explodes into a thousand splinters, mid-flight.  I catch a leg from the chair before it meets the floor, club the brawn with it - a symphony of thwacks and thumps.  As of now, the brawn was.

The door to the chamber flies open: a third hoodlum spots me sprinting for the window.  His Thompson spits a long chain of fire as I dive through glass, freefalling into the Rhine.  I swim to an imperceptible depth.  Thompson rounds slicing through the drink, zipping past me like burning bot flies.

Four hundred meters to the embankment.  I'm front-crawling in S-pull patterns - rapid acceleration, abbreviating the distance. 

A German.  Chap by the name of Biedermann is reputed for holding the record in the Men's Four Hundred Meter Long Course at three minutes, seven seconds and seven-tenths of a second.  I reach the embankment in three flat. 

Sorry, Biedermann.  Keep swimming.

I surface, climbing onto dry land - I'm a wet towel dripping salt water.  My Zapato Del Barcos squish/squash excess H20 with every step.  The leather panels remain intact.  A man who neglects to waterproof his shoes is unfit to serve. 

I vault a set of stairs, spilling onto the busy streets of Cologne, though my current odor denigrates the spirit of the city that enhanced man's fragrance. 

A shower, Roger.  Now. 

I hail a cab, tell the driver, "Grandhotel Schloss Bensberg, and hurry."  My Deutsch is flawless. 

I produce my flask, draw three swigs of Michters - champion's rye.  My blood drops ten degrees, back where I belong.

I'm at the doors of the hotel in under four minutes.  The driver receives a damp, albeit generous tip.

In my suite, shedding my shirt, trousers.  I look in the mirror, set my nose.  The pain triggers a grunt.

Nastassja lies naked on the bed, a Vogue magazine before her.  She asks how I got the bruises.

I turn for the bathroom, hurried, "None of your damned business.  Mix us a Gibson."  She stretches her legs - smooth, strong, edible.  Moves for the bar, prepares our drinks.  Her English needs practice, but it's music to my ears, "Come to bed, Roger."
 
Below the sink, I find my leather shave kit.  I unzip it, ensuring the 500-hundred year-old Ottoman Empire coins still remain - they do.  Relief.

Nastassja calls out again, anxious, "Come to bed, Roger!"

I draw hot water, peeling off the shoes, "In a moment, dear.  But first a shower."

Nastassja is at the door, holding our Gibsons like a good girl, "How about a bath?"

I take my glass, sample the martini.  Perfect.  Just like I taught her. 

I look her up and down.  My blood's up five degrees.  I crack a smile.  My eyebrow arches.  Shoot me.

I look into her eyes, starving, "Yes.  How about a bath?"

Roger, you dog.

 

The Air Max 90

Hooligans.  Filthy.  Vicious.  Out for blood.  They're chasing me.  Over a debt?   A woman?  This doesn't concern me.  The point is I'm running for my life and I'm late for drinks with Cal.

The sky's an oven.  The air smells of pure gasoline - I'm perspiring. 

And these damned trainers on my feet.  They're gripping asphalt like the claws of a mad dog.  My soles are practically on fire. 

The Air Max 90, I believe they're called. The Air Max III before the year 2K. One part mesh, one part nubuck.  It was love at first sight. 

I'm mad dashing over tin rooftops.  Homes - people live here.  Must be something, living on top of one another - Favela.  What a sight.  A district in the likeness of a staircase.  And I'm racing down, cat leaping roof-to-roof.  My heels are drilling the air capsules with every pressured drop.  And yet they gain.

I think I hear the crack of a rusted Colt.  I don't stop to check - I'm almost to the asphalt of Downtown Rio.  Three minutes ‘til drinks.

Go for broke, Roger.  You can do this.

I'm creating a sizeable gap between me and my pursuers.  Have I always been this fast?  Or are these shoes a gift from God?

A roundabout, paces ahead.  Bumper to bloody bumper.

I jump the hood of a moving Renault, skip to the windshield of a careening 5-Series.  An improvised 180 takes me to the center fountain.  I'm a spider monkey. 

And the hooligans are declining in number, bodies tumbling beneath a chain of grinding wheels.  If they're dead it's their own damn fault.

Launching into a zig-zag pattern, steady stops and pivots.  A maze of swerving vehicles - I should be dead.  But somehow I've found the sidewalk and I'm pushing through children, merchants, tourists.  Out of my way.

There's Cal, waving at me from a chair outside the cafe - thank heavens he got us a table.

A final glance behind me: no sign of the hooligans.  Better trade seats with Cal, just to be sure - his vantage point will keep me on alert.  Eyes down -

My trainers are spotless.  Not a scratch.  Money well spent…
