NECK HAIR

      In the basement of the old Hotel Sylvania building there's a barbershop.  It's been there for 89 years, but Salvatore hasn't been there that long, he chuckles as he tells me.  Sal's been there for 26 years.  Since before the building was converted into condos that go for six figures.  Before the porn theaters left in favor of BYOB restaurants, upscale house-wares and all-natural artisan soaps.  Before the neighborhood caught the name Midtown Village.

      It's a barbershop; there are three chairs, one is Sal's, the other is Joe's, the owner, next to each other on the west wall.  Joe bought the place recently and looks younger than Sal, but the close-cropped hair he has left is gray and contrasts with his thick, black-rimmed glasses.  On the north wall, facing the street, is another chair, a sink in front of it.  A white towel with a thin blue stripe down the middle hangs off the sink, half in and half out of the basin.  To the right of the sink is a shelf with a mirrored cabinet door.  This sink and chair are not used.  All that's in the shelf with the mirrored cabinet door and under it the small nook to put your tools and under two drawers, side by side, is a dusty sign that looks like it hung on the wall when every man coming in here still wore a hat. 

      A gentleman needs a good haircut, everyone knows that.  But who knows what a good haircut is? Does it cost a princely sum?  Or do you perform it yourself? Getting a haircut should be a relaxing, restorative experience.  If you prefer, and have the means, to go to a luxury spa for manicure, pedicure, facial, massage, the works, then by all means, do it.  But a man without the means or inclination for luxury should feel no less pampered by the simple pleasure of devoting time to look your best.  Proper grooming is essential for a man interested in being a gentleman. 

      Should you choose to go the professional route and have someone other than yourself cut your hair, great care should be taken in selecting a barber.  First you must consider the price.  Most men get their haircut every month to month and a half.  You must find an establishment that fits your wallet.  The last thing you should fret about is how you will pay for a good haircut.  Once you find a place whose price is reasonable, make sure that it fits with your personality.  What do I mean by this?  If their website, and they should have a website for crying out loud it’s twenty goddamn ten!, proclaims that they seek to reach new heights of art with every scissor stroke and lists a clientele of prominent politicos and business elite, think to yourself if this is really the company you would like to keep.  If you’re striving for that corner office or seat on city council, then this may be the perfect place for you to do a little casual networking. But in the end you’ll have to go there and get a cut to really get a feel for the place.
 
      Some months ago I was in search of a barber.  A friend who had loyally cut my hair for years was moving outside of the city and it would be much less convenient for her to cut my hair.  Being a man who likes convenience, I started looking around for alternatives.  I remembered a fancy salon in a very expensive neighborhood where some years ago I had received, free of charge, one of the best haircuts of my life.  It is a salon with a modern, industrial décor and a large ego.  I made an appointment, not worrying about the sixty-five dollar price, because I considered it a present to myself.  My first “real” haircut in years.  I sat in the chair after having my hair shampooed and rinsed and was horrified.  My stylist had a terrible case of butterfingers and kept dropping his scissors, which necessitated a dunk in barbicide each time.  His comb broke.  His tattooed, clammy hands reeked of stale cigarettes and the cut I received didn’t even come close to the one I received years earlier.  I paid, left a standard gratuity, and left, feeling good about having less hair, but certain I would never return.

      About two months passed and I was desperately in need of another cut.  I began searching reviews online and found a place that seemed right up my alley.  The descriptions people gave of their experiences were glowing, and they played jazz in the barbershop!  I called up to see if I could get an appointment that day.  A man with an Italian accent answered the phone and told me, “just come by at six, we’ll fit you in.”  I arrived and stepped down into the basement to a scene from another time.  Leather barber chairs, bright tile gleaming white and sure enough, jazz piping through the speakers.  The icing on the cake, though, was the straight razor the barber used on the back of my neck.  This implement is what separates the good from the great.  It takes a normal haircut from something merely functional; the shortening and removal of unwanted lengths of head hair, to the luxurious, the manly.  It is part of the craft.  Anyone can run a buzzer over the back of your neck.  But how many can wield a straight razor with such precision?  I tell you I’ve never felt more like a gentleman than after Sal wiped the shaving cream off the back of my neck.  I gave Sal 20 dollars for the cut and a five for a tip and I can safely say that that cut was more than 3 times as good as the previous cut I’d paid around 70 dollars for.  I had found my barbershop.

      Now my neck was smooth, rubbing the back of it was wonderful and I never wanted that feeling to go away.  But I knew it would.  What does one do when the inevitable neck hair begins to return? Most men live with some degree of neck hair and I am no exception.  The hair on the back of my neck is thinner and lighter in color than that of my head and face.  It is wispy, golden and somewhat of a nuisance but generally tolerable.  There is almost no better sensation than feeling my neck after a haircut, smooth and hairless, a bare stubble pulling back against the palm of my hand.  But after this moment of a hairless back-neck, each day brings me closer to a point where that soft hair has formed two curly puffs below my hairline and above my shirt collar.  I look in the mirror and I can see the brambles poking around the side of my neck. 

      A gentleman should use a beard trimmer or some similar implement to keep his neck hair in check.  A swift up, down, back and forth is all he needs to tame an unsightly and ungentlemanly crop of neck hair without resorting to too frequent trips to the barber.  Being a gentleman is not only about looking your best, or as close to your best as you can reasonably muster, but also about living within your means and even achieving some degree of frugality.  Even if a gentleman has the means to see a barber every week or every other week, should he?  No. That 20 or 30 dollars (or more if they’ve really got you by the asshole) could be much better spent.  Taking the time to do certain things for yourself, like trimming your neck hair, will put more money in your pocket and let you feel better about buying a young lady an expensive cocktail or two, or splurging on some high quality ingredients for a fabulous dinner with close friends. When a fellow gentleman asks at a small gathering you are hosting for no reason in particular, “How much do I owe you for the beer?” you can look at him assuredly and say, “Don’t worry about it.  I trim my own neck hair.”

      For men with shoulder length or longer hair, the presence of neck hair is generally not a concern because the ungentlemanly length of their hair obscures it.  Why is long hair ungentlemanly, you may ask?  For many, many reasons but let me describe the most pertinent.  First, as any gentleman who has ever shared a bathroom with a long-haired lady will tell you, long hair is disgusting.  After even one shower, he will find the tub drain cover caked with soapy, clumpy hair and, if she has not taken the lady-like step of removing her head-sheddings, he will be forced to lest the gentleman wish to stand in a ankle-deep pool of his own filth.  Second, are you really going to put a lady’s hair elastic in your hair, thereby creating a ponytail?  Really?  Ha!  Oh, I can imagine it now: “Darling, I’m trying to iron my shirt for dinner this evening but my long, lady-like hair keeps getting in the way of my face and I’m afraid it will get singed on the iron.  Could you fetch me one of those elastics you use to put your hair back when you’re performing fellatio on me?”  The list could go on and on but suffice to say a gentleman should have a neat, reasonable crop of hair that doesn’t require him to use an implement commonly employed in cock-sucking.
