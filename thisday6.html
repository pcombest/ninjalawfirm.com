On the wall someone has written my one true love. She put the cigarette between her lips and waited for the man to light it for her. She had a shiny, olive-complexion and mahogany-colored hair. Someone opened the back door of the car and she got in silently. They glided along the deserted streets of a residential neighborhood. It was the time of the year when most of the houses were empty. The man parked on a narrow street of single-story houses with identical yards. She went into the bathroom and he made coffee. The kitchen had brown tiles patterned with arabesques, and looked like a gym. She opened the curtains, there were no lights in any of the houses across the street. She took off her satin dress and the man lit another cigarette for her. Before she pulled down her underpants, the man arranged her on all fours on the soft white rug. She heard him look for something in the wardrobe. A wardrobe built into the wall, a red wardrobe. She watched him upside down, through her legs. The man smiled at her. Now someone is walking down a street where cars are parked only next to their respective lairs. Above the street, like a hanged man, swings the spot-lit sign of the neighborhood's best restaurant, closed a long time ago. Footsteps vanish down the street, headlights are visible in the distance. She said no. She listens. There's someone outside. The man went over to the window, then came back naked toward the bed. She was tan, minus where her underpants usually reside, and sometimes she pretended to be asleep. He looked at her from the door with a detached sweetness. There are silences made just for us. He pressed his face against hers until it hurt and pushed himself into her with a single thrust. Maybe she screamed a little. From the street however, nothing could be heard. 6.23.10

It was an old haunt she hadn't entered for over 8 years, and it was definitely not daytime the last time she was there. Never before 11 she would say to her girlfriends.
The early afternoon sun spilled in through the thick blinds, casting a noirish tint to the red booths and bar stools. She needed to make a call and figured she could do so in private here since the only accompaniment was fragmented sunshine and a 'busy cleaning the lines' bartender (she used to be friendly with every bartender in the place, this one she had never seen, she didn't feel like asking if Charlie, Chuck, or Yves was still around).
She made her call from a rotary phone the bartender provided from under the oak, horseshoe-shaped bar. Payphone is broken, he said as he sat the phone on the counter. Thanks, she said. The bartender pushed through a swinging door and disappeared. She dialed the number, stared at an almost empty bottle of J.T.S. Brown and thought of Paul for perhaps only the second or third time since they filmed that movie together, a few doors down, at the billiards hall.
